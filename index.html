Monday, June 13th

I'm backlogging a little (it's actually the 20th today) because this is a story of its own that is very important I write about here, but don't want to combine with the routine to-dos because that's separate.

Update: As I'm writing it, I notice it's actually quite long, so I'll go ahead and split it in three parts, one for each day.

The story is about how I ended up moderating stages at the WeAreDevelopers World Congress 2022 and how that went.

I first heard about this conference back in December I think, when Deni from TPOW (my his discord study community) wanted to apply for being a speaker. I was of course mindblown by the scale of the event, the speakers that have been there in previous years (even I had heard of them lol) and was super excited that it's going to be held in Berlin! Which is where I am!

Fast forward a few months, Deni ended up getting hired by WeAreDevs, handling social medias and helping with the organization of the event. Crazy how that escalated, good job man, I'm proud of you!

I initially signed up as a volunteer. I had no way to afford a proper ticket and I was going to be way out of my depth anyway, so I thought it'd be great to have something to do and a direct way to meet people. Besides, I've gotten so much help from the tech community, I thought this would be a lovely way to give something back.

When I told Deni I'd been accepted as a volunteer, he was like "but no wait, here's a code for a ticket and then you can just hang out with me while I do social media stuff!" - yes please and thank you :D I got my ticket and waited patiently for the event.

On the weekend right before it, he wrote me that they're a bit short on moderators and that I should do it. "It's no big deal" he said, "You can totally do it", he said. I had no idea what a moderator does and he told me I'd just have to ask the speakers a few questions after their talk, like "how did it go", "how was the audience" etc. and that' he'd probably be there too.

Well, that I can indeed do, sign me up!

On Monday the 13th I received an email from Gerry with the "moderators briefing" doc. As I was reading the eight pages of it, I slowly began to realize how much Deni had undersold the assignment to me and oversold me to Gerry.

Hol' up! I am assigned to two stages. I'm spending a full day at each stage. I'm getting up on that stage first to make all the event-related announcements. And then I'm also introducing each of the speakers for that stage. By myself too, Deni had other responsibilities. Wait, whaaaat??

Well. It was the day before the event. I was invited for a rehearsal in a few hours. I'd agreed to do it, Deni had convinced Gerry, Gerry had graciously allowed me to do it. There was no turning back, I was commited.

I rolled up my metaphorical sleeves and started familiarizing myself with the speakers and their topics as instructed in the briefing.

Automotive OS. Cloud Native Data Management. Kubernetes. SonarQube. Reactive microservices. Serverless landscape. Headless commerce. I don't know what any of those words mean. I don't know who any of those people are, and they all sound wildly impressive.

Information overload doesn't even begin to cover it.

It was becoming apparent I probably won't be able to remember all of that and it's likely I won't have time to write it all down either.

I went to the rehearsal, to at least glimpse what I was getting myself into.

The event space was massive. There were countless stages, people buzzing about setting things up, people already piling up to check in.

I was relieved to find my stage for the first day was one of the cosiest and least intimidating looking ones and felt a bit of relief. Rike, the stage manager, was very on top of things, knew what was going on and had answers to all of my questions. Her presence definitely put me more at ease. Then I met Johannes, the first of the speakers I was introducing. Luckily, he was going to talk about git and code reviews, which was one of the few comprehensible for me topics. It was his first in-person talk too, so it felt very comforting to start the experience together.

The rehearsal didn't last long, so I then went to help out with some of the checkins at the door.

In the evening I, as a moderator, was invited to the speakers' welcoming drinks at the hotel. I was mostly excited to hang out with Deni - he was my first internet-stranger-turned-friend from my coding journey and he'd done so much for me, including getting me this insane opportunity, so I was looking forward to get the chance to meet in person. Besides, meeting people is kind of the point of these conferences, isn't it, so this get together seemed like a must.

I kind of wish I could've split in two and also gone home to do homework, but that's not how it works. I was hoping by meeting some of the speakers, I'd have an easier time with introducing them later.

And it did help! I was lucky enough to chat with Johannes some more, and meet Noaa and Michael who were also at my stages. I met Marc too, who was a more experienced moderator (and a speaker) and was kind enough to reassure me a bit.

All in all, good times. On my way home I thought "it's ok, I got this, it'll be fun!"

I set myself a deadline of 2am and kept reading about the speakers and their topics before eventually getting to bed.

My key takeaways from the first three classes:

  • Separation of concerns - I loved this way of putting it and the idea of compartmentalizing everything. HTML is for content, CSS is for style and JS is for function.
  • I loved the peanut m&ms analogy too - The peanut is the essence, which is HTML. The chocolate is a bonus, which is CSS and then there’s a thin coating to pimp it up (I personally know the m&ms coating’s true purpose and it sure isn’t an “optional” thing, but I’ll let it slide lol), which is JavaScript. For the sake of accessibility even when the internet is shit, it’s important to focus on the essence and keep it light on the bonus and pimping it up parts.
  • Each tag has its purpose. Its purpose should never be style.
  • The “head” contains the information that is for the browser. The “body” is what is shown to the user. The “you can see my sexy body but you can’t see what’s inside my head” was hilarious and a great way to remember it.
  • Drawing out the elements of a webpage and deconstructing it goes a long way towards understanding what to use where.
  • The tags themselves are easy to lookup and use (mdn is a friend)
